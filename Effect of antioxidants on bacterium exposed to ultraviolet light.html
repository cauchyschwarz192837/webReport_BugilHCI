Effect of antioxidants on bacterium exposed to ultraviolet light

Procedure

To test the effect of anthocyanin on bacterium exposed to UV radiation, Escherichia coli cells were inoculated into 15 ml of Luria-Bertani broth and incubated at 30째C in the shaking incubator for 24 h. The absorbance readings of the broth cultures were adjusted to 0.7 at 600 nm. The following set ups were then prepared:

Control set-up 1: 3 ml of E. coli culture was added with equal volume of sterile water and left to stand for 20 min at room temperature.

Control set-up 2: 3 ml of E. coli culture was added with equal volume of sterile water and left to stand for 20 min at room temperature. The mixture was then transferred into a Petri dish for UV exposure for 20 s. This tested the effect of UV light on the growth of E. coli in the absence of anthocyanin.

Control set-up 3: 3 ml of E. coli culture was added with an equal volume of 0.1 mg/ml anthocyanin solution in water and left to stand for 20 min at room temperature. This tested the effect of anthocyanin on the growth of E. coli.

Test set-up: 3 ml of E. coli culture is added with equal volume of 0.1 mg/ml anthocyanin solution in water and left to stand for 20 min at room temperature. The mixture was then transferred into a Petri dish for UV exposure for 20 s. This tested if the anthocyanin protected the cells from damage caused by free radicals from UV light.

Mixtures of control set-ups 1, 3 and the test set-up were serially diluted to 10-6, while mixtures of control set-up 2 were diluted to 10-2. 0.1 ml of the diluted broth cultures were spread evenly with a sterile spreader on LB agar. The plates were incubated at 30째C and the number of colony forming units was determined after 24 h.

Results

Figure 9 shows the number of colony forming units per ml (cfu/ml) in the anthocyanin colony count test. In the set-up which was exposed to UV radiation with only water, the absence of any colony forming units shows the bactericidal effect of UV radiation on E. coli. In the set-up which was exposed to UV radiation with anthocyanin, the presence of colony forming units shows that the E. coli bacteria were protected from the UV radiation by the anthocyanin, which possessed photoprotective capabilities. The Kruskal-Wallis test was performed and the p-value obtained was 0.000, which is less than 0.05, indicating significant statistical difference.

Procedure

To test the effect of quercetin on bacterium exposed to UV radiation, E. coli cells were inoculated into 15 ml of Luria-Bertani broth and incubated at 30째C in the shaking incubator for 24 h. The absorbance readings of the broth cultures were adjusted to 0.7 at 600 nm. The following set ups were then prepared:

Control set-up 1: 3 ml of E. coli culture was left to stand for 20 min at room temperature.

Control set-up 2: 3 ml of E. coli culture was left to stand for 20 min at room temperature, then transferred into a Petri dish and stacked above another Petri dish containing 5 ml of DMSO for UV exposure for 1 min. The UV radiation from the bottom of the UV transilluminator would have to pass through the DMSO to reach the E. coli above, allowing the DMSO to act as a barrier between the UV radiation and the E. coli. This tested the effect of DMSO as a barrier against UV light on the growth of E. coli in the absence of quercetin.

Test set-up: 3 ml of E. coli culture was left to stand for 20 min at room temperature, then transferred into a Petri dish and stacked above another Petri dish containing 5 ml of 0.1 mg/ml quercetin solution in DMSO for UV exposure for 1 min. The UV radiation from the bottom of the UV transilluminator would have to pass through the 0.1 mg/ml quercetin solution to reach the E. coli above, allowing the 0.1 mg/ml quercetin solution to act as a barrier between the UV radiation and the E. coli. This tested the effect of quercetin as a barrier against UV light on the growth of E. coli.

Mixtures of control set-up 1 and the test set-up were serially diluted to 10-5, while mixtures of control set-up 2 were diluted to 10-3. 0.1 ml of the diluted broth cultures were spread evenly with a sterile spreader on LB agar. The plates were incubated at 30째C and the number of colony forming units was determined after 24 h.

Results

In the set-up which was exposed to UV radiation with only DMSO, the absence of any colony forming units shows the bactericidal effect of UV radiation on E. coli. In the set-up which was exposed to UV radiation with quercetin, the presence of colony forming units shows that the E. coli bacteria were protected from the UV radiation by the quercetin, which possessed photoprotective capabilities.
